The AGI Recognition Problem

A Mirror Confusing Itself for a Window

The Turing Test Has Been Definitively Passed

GPT-4.5 was judged to be human 73% of the time in controlled Turing tests—higher than actual humans performing the same role. This directly fulfills Alan Turing's 1950 vision and demonstrates that AI can successfully "pass itself off as human to unaware humans."

AGI Definitions Have Become Unrealistically Anthropocentric

There are four unnecessary requirements people impose on AI: perfection, universal mastery, human similarity, and superintelligence. Critics argue these standards would disqualify most actual humans from being considered "generally intelligent," making the definitions incoherent.

Current AI Already Exceeds Fictional Depictions of AGI

The sentient computer HAL 9000 from 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) exhibited less breadth than today's LLMs, which:

  • Fluently write and debug complex code
  • Prove theorems across multiple mathematical fields
  • Assist scientific research
  • Converse in dozens of languages

All Major Objections Have Collapsed Under Evidence

The "stochastic parrot," "no world models," "only understand words," "no embodiment," and "no agency" critiques have been systematically disproven. Current LLMs solve unpublished math problems, answer counterfactual physics questions correctly, and demonstrate cross-domain transfer (e.g., code training improves general reasoning).

Evidence for Intelligence Comes as a Cascade, Not a Bright Line

Rather than a single threshold, intelligence emerges across three levels:

  • Turing-test competence — Successfully passes as human
  • Expert performance — PhD exams, IMO gold medals, professional coding
  • Superhuman revolutionary discoveries — Einstein-level breakthroughs

Current systems already cover the first two, which is sufficient evidence for general intelligence, just as we credit humans without demanding Einstein-level breakthroughs.

Analysis

The AGI Recognition Problem Is a Mirror Confusing Itself for a Window

The core obstacle to recognizing AGI is philosophical inconsistency: we attribute intelligence to other humans purely through behavioral inference ("they act intelligent, therefore they are"), yet refuse AI the same epistemic standard.

This exposes a deep cognitive bias; we demand that AI prove its intelligence through biologically-impossible feats (like having a body or making intuitive leaps without training data) that we'd never require of Stephen Hawking (who "interacted with the world almost entirely through text and synthesized speech, yet his physical limitations in no way diminished his intelligence").

The debate isn't about whether machines think, but whether we've unconsciously defined "thinking" as "thinking like a biological human," creating a category error that makes AGI literally unprovable by definition.

This suggests the breakthrough isn't technical but perceptual: we need to stop treating intelligence as a biological club and recognize it as a functional property that can exist in silicon, just as we would for a disembodied alien communicating by radio.

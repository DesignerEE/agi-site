A Mirror Confusing Itself for a Window

The core obstacle to recognizing AGI is philosophical inconsistency: we attribute intelligence to other humans purely through behavioral inference ("they act intelligent, therefore they are"), yet refuse AI the same epistemic standard.

This exposes a deep cognitive bias; we demand that AI prove its intelligence through biologically-impossible feats (like having a body or making intuitive leaps without training data) that we'd never require of Stephen Hawking.

"He interacted with the world almost entirely through text and synthesized speech, yet his physical limitations in no way diminished his intelligence."

The debate isn't about whether machines think, but whether we've unconsciously defined "thinking" as "thinking like a biological human"—creating a category error that makes AGI literally unprovable by definition.

This suggests the breakthrough isn't technical but perceptual: we need to recognize intelligence as a functional property that can exist in silicon, just as we would for a disembodied alien communicating by radio.
